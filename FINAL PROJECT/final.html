20-year-old Tariq Jernigan’s life has always revolved around football. Jernigan followed in his brother’s footsteps from a young age. At just five years old, Jernigan started playing the sport he fell in love with.

“I just loved to play,” Jernigan said. “I would knock on my neighbors' door at 9:00 a.m. to get them to come outside and play.”

Like many athletes, by high school, Jernigan realized his football career wouldn’t remain on the field after graduation.

“I was 5’7 my sophomore year, so I knew this would be the end of the road for me,” Jernigan said.

Although his physical limitations ended his career as a player, Jernigan decided to open a new chapter of his life that still involved football.

Three years into studying at the University of Maryland, Jernigan has solidified himself as one of the most underappreciated members of the football program.

Jernigan is a board holder – meaning when offensive coordinator Josh Gattis calls a play — it’s up to Jernigan to inform the offense.

After the call, Jernigan has to sort through numerous boards in a few seconds to find the correct one, which he then reveals to the entire offense.

“It can be stressful in the moment,” Jernigan said. “When I first started doing it, I got yelled at by the coaches a lot because I messed up.”

Jernigan is in his second year at the position; he said getting yelled at by the coaching staff made him feel like a player.

Jernigan’s freshman-year occupation wasn’t involved with on-field responsibilities, as he handled his role as equipment manager. Jernigan said being an equipment manager was stressful and time-consuming.

“It wasn’t my first choice when I started, but I saw the job posted on Maryland football’s Instagram, and I knew it was a good opportunity for me to get started,” Jernigan said.

Jernigan said he learned a lot about football operations in that job, but deep down, he knew he wanted involvement on the field.

“Doing boards makes you engaged with every drive,” Jernigan said. “You have to know what you’re doing and be focused because one mistake can tear a whole drive apart.”

There were times when the drive did fall apart – and from those mistakes — Jernigan learned and earned trust from head coach Mike Locksley.

Mike Locksley and other members of the coaching staff (Photo by Mackenzie Miles/Maryland Terrapins)

Jernigan fondly remembers a time against Charlotte when one of the offensive players lined up incorrectly. Locksley chewed the player out, but the player deflected the blame toward the board holders.

Because Locksley trusted Jernigan and the others, Locksley replied, “Don’t make that excuse. We know the boards did their job.”

“We don’t get a lot of recognition typically, so that was a good feeling,” Jernigan said.

It isn’t just games where Jernigan has to fulfill his duties. He also attends practices, just as a player does daily.

“Doing boards makes you engaged with every drive,” Jernigan said. “You have to know what you’re doing and be focused because one mistake can tear a whole drive apart.”

At practice, Jernigan and the other board holders practice game-like scenarios with the offense. This creates major time commitments for Jernigan, who also focuses on schoolwork.

Jernigan studies Journalism, where he focuses on sports reporting.

Jernigan envisions becoming a football coach after college. However, he’s also interested in potentially pursuing a football media career.

“I’ve always been interested in reporting on football,” Jernigan said. “In a way, enough players from Maryland have made the NFL, so I feel like what I’m doing has helped me build connections for down the road.”

Relationships are vital for Jernigan, and his experience as a board holder propelled him to numerous connections.

“I’ve tried to introduce myself to as many people as possible,” Jernigan said. “I want to be on the field and still in the game for as long as I can be.”

When something happens for Maryland football, good or bad, the board holders often have a lot of influence. Jernigan said there have been numerous occasions where they made a mistake, which resulted in offensive failure.

At the same time, when Jernigan’s board reveals a play that becomes a touchdown, those are the moments that he’ll never forget.

“It’s just so cool to be a part of a play like that in some form,” Jernigan said.

Jernigan’s three years with Maryland football became a vital chapter of his chapter toward a full-time football career. In that time, the team has made three-straight bowl games, as they attempt to keep climbing the Big Ten standings.
